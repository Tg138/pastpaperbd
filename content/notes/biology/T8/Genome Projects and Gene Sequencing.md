# Genome Projects and Gene Sequencing

*AQA spec ref: 3.8.3 — Genome projects and gene sequencing*

A **genome** is the complete set of genetic information of an organism — every DNA base pair in every chromosome, including protein-coding genes, non-coding regulatory sequences, introns, and repetitive DNA. **Genome projects** are large-scale scientific efforts to determine the full DNA sequence of a species' genome. The most famous is the **Human Genome Project (HGP)**, completed in 2003 after 13 years of international collaboration: it mapped all ~3 billion base pairs of the human genome and identified approximately 20,000–25,000 protein-coding genes.

Since then, the development of **next-generation sequencing (NGS)** technologies has made sequencing faster and cheaper by orders of magnitude. Genomes of thousands of species have now been sequenced, from bacteria to plants to mammals.

## What Genome Sequencing Reveals

Knowing the complete DNA sequence of an organism allows:

**Identification of all genes** — once the sequence is known, coding regions can be identified computationally (by searching for start codons, stop codons, promoter sequences, and splice sites). This gives a complete catalogue of potential proteins the organism can produce.

**Comparison between organisms** — comparing genome sequences across species reveals:
- **Evolutionary relationships**: species that share more DNA sequence similarity are more closely related. This provides an independent, molecular line of evidence for phylogeny (complementing anatomical and fossil evidence). Even distantly related organisms share ancient conserved sequences (e.g. genes involved in DNA replication or ribosome function), reflecting common ancestry.
- **Gene function**: if a gene sequence in a newly sequenced organism is very similar to a gene of known function in another organism (homologous sequence), its function can be inferred. This is particularly powerful for model organisms (e.g. yeast, *Drosophila*, *C. elegans*, mouse) where many genes have been characterised experimentally.
- **Non-coding regions**: comparing sequences reveals which regions are highly conserved (suggesting functional importance) and which vary freely (suggesting neutral DNA). This has revealed that many regulatory sequences outside coding regions are conserved.

## The Genome-to-Proteome Problem

The **proteome** is the full range of proteins that a cell or organism is capable of producing. In principle, knowing the genome should give you the proteome. In practice, the relationship is complex:

### Simple Organisms

In bacteria and other prokaryotes, the genome-to-proteome relationship is relatively straightforward:
- Most genes are continuously expressed (few introns, little alternative splicing)
- Regulatory regions are simple and predictable
- A known sequence → predictable protein product with reasonable confidence

This has immediate practical applications. For example, sequencing the genome of a pathogen (bacterium, virus, or parasite) allows scientists to identify surface proteins unique to that organism — potential **vaccine antigens** or **drug targets** — without growing and handling dangerous organisms.

### Complex Organisms

In eukaryotes — particularly vertebrates — the genome-to-proteome relationship is far more complex:

**Alternative splicing** — most human genes contain multiple exons. The pre-mRNA can be spliced in different ways (different exon combinations), generating many different proteins from a single gene. The human genome of ~20,000 genes can produce an estimated 100,000+ different proteins through alternative splicing alone. The genome predicts what proteins *could* be made; which ones *are* made depends on cell type and developmental stage.

**Non-coding DNA** — a large fraction of eukaryotic genomes does not code for protein. This includes:
- Introns (removed by splicing before translation)
- Regulatory sequences (promoters, enhancers, silencers)
- Repetitive sequences (satellite DNA, transposons)
- RNA-coding genes (ribosomal RNA, tRNA, microRNA)

Much of this non-coding DNA regulates gene expression in ways not yet fully understood. Its presence means the genome is far larger than a simple catalogue of protein sequences.

**Post-translational modifications** — proteins are often modified after synthesis (phosphorylation, glycosylation, cleavage) in ways not directly encoded by the DNA sequence. The proteome includes these modified forms; the genome alone cannot predict them.

The result: even with a complete genome sequence, we cannot predict the full proteome of a complex eukaryote from sequence data alone. Proteomics (the large-scale study of proteins directly) is needed alongside genomics.

## Applications of Genome Sequencing

**Vaccine development** — sequencing pathogens (bacteria, viruses) identifies unique surface proteins (antigens) that the immune system can recognise. These can be used as vaccine targets without needing to grow live pathogen. This is particularly valuable for dangerous pathogens (e.g. identifying antigens of SARS-CoV-2 from its sequenced genome enabled rapid vaccine development).

**Gene therapy** — knowing which gene is mutated in a genetic disease (identified by genome sequencing of affected individuals) allows development of targeted gene therapies. The healthy version of the gene can be introduced into cells to correct the defect.

**Drug development** — genome sequences reveal all the proteins an organism (or cancer) produces. Novel drug targets can be identified: proteins essential to the pathogen (or cancer cell) but absent or very different in the host. Drugs designed to inhibit these proteins will be selective.

**Genetic screening** — identifying disease-associated alleles (discovered through sequencing affected populations) allows predictive genetic testing for inherited conditions.

**Forensic and identification applications** — DNA profiling, identification of individuals, animal/plant species identification in forensics or food fraud.

**Evolutionary biology** — comparing genomes across species builds molecular phylogenies, refines our understanding of evolutionary relationships, and identifies the genetic basis of adaptations.

## Ethical, Financial and Social Issues

Genome sequencing raises issues that AQA expects you to recognise and evaluate:

**Privacy and data use** — an individual's genome reveals predispositions to inherited diseases. Who has the right to access this information? Employers? Insurance companies? Governments? Inappropriate access could lead to discrimination.

**Genetic determinism** — genome sequencing can create a misleading impression that genes determine destiny. In reality, most traits result from complex gene-environment interactions. Overstating genetic information can stigmatise individuals or ethnic groups.

**Financial costs** — large-scale sequencing projects require enormous investment. Debate exists about whether public funding should prioritise genome projects over other medical research or healthcare provision.

**Genetically modified organisms (GMOs)** — genome knowledge enables more precise genetic modification of crops and animals. This raises questions about ecological risk (gene transfer to wild relatives, effects on non-target species), corporate ownership of genomes, and public acceptance.

**Gene therapy ethics** — somatic gene therapy (correcting genes in non-reproductive cells) is generally more accepted. Germ-line gene therapy (editing embryos so changes are heritable) raises profound ethical questions about designer genetics, consent of future generations, and inequitable access to enhancement technologies.

## Summary

- **Genome project**: determines the complete DNA sequence of an organism's genome; Human Genome Project (2003) sequenced ~3 billion bp of human DNA
- **What sequencing reveals**: all genes and regulatory sequences; evolutionary relationships (by genome comparison); gene function (by homology to known genes); vaccine and drug targets
- **Simple organisms**: genome → proteome relationship fairly direct; sequence can predict protein products; useful for identifying vaccine antigens and drug targets
- **Complex organisms**: alternative splicing → multiple proteins per gene (proteome >> genome); non-coding regulatory DNA; post-translational modifications; genome cannot fully predict proteome
- **Applications**: vaccines, gene therapy, drug targets, genetic screening, forensics, evolutionary biology
- **Ethical issues**: privacy, genetic discrimination, financial priorities, GMO concerns, germ-line therapy

## AQA Exam Tips

- **Genome vs proteome**: AQA often asks why the proteome is larger than the genome. Answer: alternative splicing — one gene can produce multiple different mRNA sequences and thus multiple different proteins.
- **Simple vs complex organisms**: for simple organisms (bacteria), genome → proteome is relatively direct; for complex eukaryotes it is not, because of non-coding DNA and alternative splicing. Always state which organism type you are discussing.
- **Vaccine development from genome**: the key point is that surface protein (antigen) sequences can be identified from genome data and used to produce a vaccine without culturing the live pathogen — important for safety with dangerous organisms.
- **Evolutionary relationships from genome comparison**: this is an independent line of evidence — genome similarity correlates with evolutionary relatedness, which provides molecular evidence for phylogeny separate from anatomy or the fossil record.
- **Ethical issues**: AQA asks you to evaluate — give balanced points (benefits of research vs risks of misuse; benefits of genetic testing vs risks to privacy/insurance). Never present ethics as one-sided.
- **No detail on sequencing methods**: AQA does not require knowledge of chain termination (Sanger) or next-generation sequencing methodology. Knowing that methods are automated and continually improving is sufficient.
