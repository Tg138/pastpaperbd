# Nutrient Cycles

*AQA spec ref: 3.5.4 — Nutrient cycles and microorganisms*

Carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus move continuously through ecosystems in cycles driven largely by microorganisms. Unlike energy (which enters as sunlight and is lost as heat at each trophic level), nutrients are **recycled** — the same atoms pass through organisms, soils, water, and the atmosphere repeatedly. Microorganisms are the engine of this recycling: without them, nutrients would accumulate in dead matter and become unavailable to producers.

## Saprobionts and Saprobiotic Nutrition

**Saprobionts** (also called decomposers or saprotrophic organisms) are heterotrophs that feed on dead organic matter — the bodies of dead organisms and their waste products. They include:
- Fungi (e.g. *Mucor*, *Penicillium*)
- Bacteria (e.g. *Bacillus subtilis*)

Saprobionts carry out **saprobiotic nutrition**:
1. They secrete **extracellular digestive enzymes** (proteases, lipases, cellulases, amylases) onto the dead material
2. Large, insoluble organic molecules are broken down into small, soluble products
3. These products are absorbed by the saprobiont by diffusion or active transport

This extracellular digestion is essential because saprobionts have no internal digestive system. The breakdown of complex organic molecules (proteins → amino acids → ammonium; nucleic acids → nitrogenous bases; carbohydrates → CO₂ and water) releases inorganic nutrients back into the soil and atmosphere, making them available to producers again.

## Mycorrhizae

**Mycorrhizae** are mutualistic associations between fungi and the roots of most vascular plants. The fungal hyphae extend far beyond the root's reach, massively increasing the surface area available for absorption.

The fungus benefits by receiving carbohydrates (sugars) from the plant. The plant benefits because the fungal hyphae:
- Absorb **water** more efficiently from a larger soil volume
- Absorb **inorganic ions** — particularly phosphate (PO₄³⁻), nitrate (NO₃⁻), and potassium — from soil pores too small for roots to reach
- Release enzymes that free phosphate from organic molecules in the soil

Mycorrhizae are particularly important in nutrient-poor soils. Most crop plants and forest trees have mycorrhizal associations. Their disruption (by fungicides or soil disturbance) can significantly reduce plant growth.

## The Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen makes up 78% of the atmosphere, but N₂ gas is inert and unavailable to plants or animals. The nitrogen cycle converts atmospheric N₂ into forms organisms can use, and returns it to the atmosphere. All the key conversions are carried out by bacteria.

### Nitrogen Fixation

**Nitrogen fixation** converts atmospheric N₂ into ammonium ions (NH₄⁺), which can be absorbed by plants.

- **Mutualistic fixation**: *Rhizobium* bacteria live in nodules on the roots of leguminous plants (peas, beans, clover). They receive carbohydrates from the plant and supply fixed nitrogen in return. They use the enzyme **nitrogenase** to reduce N₂ → NH₄⁺ under anaerobic conditions (nitrogenase is inactivated by O₂, so leghaemoglobin in the nodule mops up O₂).
- **Free-living fixation**: *Azotobacter* and cyanobacteria (e.g. *Anabaena*) fix nitrogen independently in soil or water.

### Ammonification

Dead organisms and animal waste contain nitrogen in organic form — proteins, nucleic acids, urea. **Saprobiotic bacteria and fungi** break these down by extracellular enzymes, releasing nitrogen as **ammonium ions (NH₄⁺)** into the soil. This process is **ammonification**.

### Nitrification

Plants can absorb ammonium ions, but nitrification converts them to nitrate (NO₃⁻), which is the main form absorbed by most plants.

- **Step 1**: *Nitrosomonas* bacteria oxidise NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ (nitrite)
- **Step 2**: *Nitrobacter* bacteria oxidise NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (nitrate)

These are **chemoautotrophs** — they use the energy released by these oxidation reactions to fix CO₂ and synthesise organic molecules. Nitrification requires aerobic conditions — waterlogged soils suppress it.

### Assimilation

Plants absorb nitrate (NO₃⁻) and ammonium (NH₄⁺) ions from the soil via root hair cells (active transport, using ATP). In the plant, these nitrogen-containing ions are used to synthesise amino acids, proteins, nucleotides, and nucleic acids. Animals obtain nitrogen by consuming plants (or other animals) and digesting proteins.

### Denitrification

**Denitrification** reverses the cycle, converting nitrate back to atmospheric N₂. Anaerobic bacteria (notably *Pseudomonas denitrificans*) use NO₃⁻ as an alternative electron acceptor when O₂ is unavailable, reducing it to N₂ gas:

NO₃⁻ → NO₂⁻ → N₂O → N₂

Denitrification occurs in **waterlogged, anaerobic soils** and reduces soil nitrogen availability. It is why waterlogged agricultural soils lose fertility rapidly.

### Summary of Nitrogen Cycle Processes

| Process | Starting material | Product | Organisms responsible |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nitrogen fixation | N₂ | NH₄⁺ | *Rhizobium*, *Azotobacter* |
| Ammonification | Organic N (proteins, urea) | NH₄⁺ | Saprobiotic bacteria/fungi |
| Nitrification | NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ | Nitrate | *Nitrosomonas*, *Nitrobacter* |
| Assimilation | NO₃⁻ / NH₄⁺ | Amino acids, proteins | Plants |
| Denitrification | NO₃⁻ | N₂ | *Pseudomonas denitrificans* |

## The Phosphorus Cycle

The phosphorus cycle is simpler than the nitrogen cycle because there is no atmospheric reservoir — phosphorus cycles between rocks, soil, water, and organisms.

- **Weathering**: phosphate (PO₄³⁻) is released from rocks by weathering and dissolves in soil water
- **Assimilation**: plants absorb phosphate ions; animals obtain phosphorus by eating plants or animals. Phosphorus is incorporated into DNA, ATP, phospholipids (cell membranes), and bone/teeth
- **Decomposition**: saprobionts break down dead organic matter, releasing phosphate back into the soil
- **Leaching**: phosphate can be washed from soil into water bodies (rivers, lakes) by rain — this is a source of eutrophication
- **Sedimentation**: phosphate that enters the sea eventually forms sedimentary rock, locking it away for geological time

Mycorrhizal fungi are particularly important for plant phosphorus uptake because phosphate is relatively immobile in soil (unlike nitrate which moves by mass flow).

## Fertilisers: Natural and Artificial

Plants remove nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium from soil as they grow. When crops are harvested, those nutrients leave the ecosystem and must be replaced.

**Natural fertilisers** (manure, compost, sewage sludge) contain complex organic molecules. Saprobionts decompose these slowly, gradually releasing inorganic ions. Benefits: slow release prevents leaching; improves soil structure; adds organic matter. Limitation: lower concentration of nutrients; variable composition.

**Artificial fertilisers** (ammonium nitrate, superphosphate, potassium chloride — NPK fertilisers) provide nutrients directly as soluble inorganic salts. Benefits: rapid plant uptake; precise nutrient ratios; high concentration. Limitation: high solubility → high risk of leaching.

## Eutrophication and Leaching

**Leaching** occurs when water washes soluble ions through the soil into rivers and lakes. Nitrate and phosphate are particularly susceptible because they are highly water-soluble. Heavy rain after fertiliser application, or application to waterlogged ground, greatly increases leaching.

**Eutrophication** is the enrichment of a water body with nutrients (primarily nitrate and phosphate), leading to a sequence of ecological damage:

1. **Algal bloom** — excess nitrate and phosphate support rapid growth and reproduction of algae and cyanobacteria at the water surface, forming a dense layer
2. **Light reduction** — the algal bloom blocks light from reaching submerged aquatic plants, which die because they cannot photosynthesise
3. **Increased decomposer activity** — dead algae and plants provide a massive supply of organic matter for aerobic decomposing bacteria, which multiply rapidly
4. **Oxygen depletion (deoxygenation)** — aerobic decomposers consume vast amounts of dissolved O₂ in their respiration. This creates a **biological oxygen demand (BOD)** that exceeds the supply of dissolved oxygen
5. **Death of aerobic organisms** — fish, invertebrates, and other aerobic organisms suffocate as dissolved O₂ falls to near zero. The water body becomes dead

The critical step students miss: it is the **decomposers** that consume the oxygen, not the algae directly. The algae die, then decomposers break them down and deplete the O₂.

## Summary

- **Saprobionts**: decomposers (bacteria/fungi) that secrete extracellular enzymes onto dead matter; absorb soluble products; release inorganic nutrients
- **Mycorrhizae**: mutualistic fungi on plant roots; increase surface area for absorption; particularly important for phosphate and water uptake
- **Nitrogen cycle**: fixation (N₂ → NH₄⁺ by *Rhizobium* / *Azotobacter*) → ammonification (organic N → NH₄⁺ by saprobionts) → nitrification (NH₄⁺ → NO₃⁻ by *Nitrosomonas* / *Nitrobacter*) → assimilation (plants absorb NO₃⁻ / NH₄⁺) → denitrification (NO₃⁻ → N₂ by *Pseudomonas*, anaerobic)
- **Phosphorus cycle**: weathering → absorption → decomposition → leaching/sedimentation
- **Natural fertilisers**: organic, slow release, low leaching risk; **artificial fertilisers**: inorganic, rapid uptake, high leaching risk
- **Eutrophication sequence**: fertiliser → leaching → algal bloom → light blocked → submerged plants die → decomposers increase → O₂ depleted → aerobic organisms die

## AQA Exam Tips

- **Nitrification requires aerobic conditions**: waterlogged soils are anaerobic, so nitrification stops. This is why waterlogged soils accumulate ammonium and why denitrification dominates — both relevant to agriculture.
- **Denitrification occurs in anaerobic conditions**: if asked why draining waterlogged fields increases soil fertility, the answer is that aerobic conditions are restored: nitrification can occur (NH₄⁺ → NO₃⁻) and denitrification is reduced (less NO₃⁻ → N₂).
- **Eutrophication — which organisms deplete O₂?** Always state it is the aerobic **decomposing bacteria** (not the algae) that consume dissolved O₂. The algae cause the problem by dying and providing organic matter.
- **Specific organism names in nitrogen cycle**: AQA does not always require specific names, but knowing *Rhizobium* (mutualistic fixation in root nodules), *Nitrosomonas*/*Nitrobacter* (nitrification), and *Pseudomonas denitrificans* (denitrification) allows precise answers and can score additional marks.
- **Mycorrhizae and phosphate**: if asked why plants without mycorrhizae grow poorly in phosphate-poor soils, explain that mycorrhizal hyphae extend the absorptive surface far beyond the root system and access phosphate in soil pores roots cannot reach.
- **No specific bacterial names needed for AQA** in general: the spec says "the role of bacteria in the nitrogen cycle" without naming specific genera. However, named examples always strengthen an answer.
