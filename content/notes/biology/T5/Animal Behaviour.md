# Animal Behaviour

*AQA spec ref: 3.5.3 — Animal behaviour: courtship and reproduction*

Animals invest significant time and energy in **courtship behaviour** — a set of ritualised signals and responses that occur before mating. Courtship is not random: it is a highly specific set of behaviours shaped by natural selection to maximise reproductive success. Understanding courtship requires you to link specific behaviours to specific outcomes and to quantify those links from data.

## The Functions of Courtship Behaviour

Courtship serves several interrelated purposes, each of which can be tested in isolation by AQA:

**Species recognition** — courtship signals (calls, displays, pheromones, dances) are species-specific. They prevent interbreeding between related species that share the same habitat, which would produce sterile or unfit hybrid offspring. Only individuals of the correct species respond appropriately to the signal.

**Assessment of mate quality** — courtship allows individuals to evaluate potential mates before committing to reproduction. Signals such as plumage brightness, call complexity, or physical endurance can advertise genetic quality, parasite resistance, or nutritional status. Choosing a high-quality mate increases the probability that offspring inherit favourable traits and survive to reproduce.

**Synchronisation of reproductive states** — many animals are only physiologically capable of reproduction at specific times (seasonal breeders, induced ovulators). Courtship signals stimulate hormonal changes in the receiving individual, synchronising the reproductive readiness of both partners. Without this synchronisation, gametes may not be produced at the right time, reducing fertilisation success.

**Pair bonding and parental investment** — in species with biparental care, courtship helps establish a bond that persists through raising offspring. This is particularly important in birds and some mammals where both parents are needed to successfully rear young.

**In summary**: more courtship success → more matings → more fertilisations → more offspring that survive → higher lifetime reproductive fitness.

## Xenopus laevis — Courtship in an Aquatic Frog

AQA uses *Xenopus laevis* (the African clawed frog) as a specific case study for the role of calls in courtship. Males produce three distinct types of vocalisations, each with a different function in the mating sequence:

**Advertisement calls** — produced by males to attract females from a distance. These calls signal the male's presence, species identity, and location. Females are attracted toward males producing advertisement calls. The number of advertisement calls correlates with the number of females approaching.

**Mating calls** — produced when a male is in physical contact with a female (amplexus) or beginning to mate. These calls function to initiate and sustain mating behaviour. An increase in mating calls is associated with increased probability of successful amplexus and egg release.

**Rasping calls** — produced by males to signal sexual inactivity or to reject unwanted contact (e.g. from another male). Rasping calls suppress further courtship attempts toward that individual.

### Interpreting Xenopus Data in Exams

AQA presents data tables showing call rates and behavioural outcomes. The expected chain of reasoning is:

1. **Identify the change**: state precisely what happened numerically (e.g. "advertisement call rate increased from X to Y when female was present")
2. **Link to behaviour**: state which specific behaviour this change caused or correlated with ("females were attracted at a higher rate")
3. **Link to reproductive success**: connect behaviour to offspring ("more females approached → more matings → more eggs fertilised → more offspring")

Vague statements ("more calls = more reproduction") will not score. AQA requires you to trace the causal chain through each step.

## How Reducing Courtship Signals Reduces Reproductive Success

This is a standard analysis question. The reasoning:

- Fewer or absent courtship signals → fewer potential mates attracted or willing to mate
- Fewer matings → fewer fertilisation events
- Fewer fertilisation events → fewer offspring produced
- Therefore: reproductive success (offspring surviving to reproduce) is reduced

This chain applies to any courtship signal — visual displays, chemical signals (pheromones), acoustic signals, or tactile behaviours. Each signal type has the same logical role: it is a necessary step in the sequence leading to fertilisation.

## Courtship in Context: Costs and Benefits

Courtship is not free. It carries costs:
- **Time** and **energy** spent on displays reduce time available for feeding, territory defence, or vigilance against predators
- **Conspicuous displays** can attract predators — elaborate plumage and loud calls are visible/audible to predators as well as to mates

Natural selection has balanced these costs against the reproductive benefits. This is why courtship signals have evolved to be specifically detectable by conspecifics (members of the same species) while minimising unnecessary predation risk — for example, many pheromones are species-specific and chemically invisible to non-target species.

## Summary

- **Courtship behaviour** precedes mating; evolved by natural selection to maximise reproductive success
- **Functions**: species recognition (prevents hybridisation), mate quality assessment (advertises genetic fitness), synchronisation of reproductive states (stimulates hormone release), pair bond formation (for biparental care)
- **Xenopus laevis calls**:
  - Advertisement calls: attract females from a distance
  - Mating calls: initiate and sustain mating behaviour
  - Rasping calls: signal sexual inactivity / reject contact
- **Reduced courtship → fewer mates → fewer fertilisations → fewer offspring**
- Courtship has costs (energy, predation risk) balanced by reproductive benefits

## AQA Exam Tips

- **Always complete the chain of reasoning**: don't stop at "fewer calls = fewer mates." You must explicitly reach "fewer offspring" or "reduced reproductive success" to score the final mark.
- **Use the data**: AQA gives you numerical data for a reason. Quote the values when explaining a trend (e.g. "when call rate fell from 30 to 8 per minute, the number of females approaching fell from 12 to 2").
- **Functions of courtship — distinguish clearly**: species recognition (prevents hybridisation) is different from mate quality assessment (chooses best mate). AQA often asks for two distinct functions — don't write the same function twice in different words.
- **Synchronisation of reproductive states** is frequently missed. It means the courtship signal triggers hormonal changes in the receiving individual so they become sexually receptive at the right time — not just that both animals are "in the mood."
- **Rasping calls**: remember that rasping signals *inactivity* or rejection — it suppresses mating attempts, it does not initiate them. Confusing rasping with mating calls is a common error.
