# Enzymes

*AQA spec ref: 3.1.4.2 — Enzymes and enzyme activity*

Enzymes are **biological catalysts** — globular proteins with a specific three-dimensional active site that binds to a complementary substrate, lowering the activation energy of a reaction and increasing its rate without being consumed. Every enzyme-catalysed reaction in the cell depends on the precise shape of the active site, which is maintained by the tertiary (and sometimes quaternary) structure of the protein. Anything that disrupts that shape destroys enzyme activity.

## The Active Site and Substrate Binding

The **active site** is a small region of the enzyme surface, usually a cleft or pocket, formed by the tertiary structure of the polypeptide. Its shape and charge distribution are complementary to the substrate molecule — not identical, but complementary (like a lock and key in shape, but with matching charge patterns too).

Two models describe how the active site interacts with the substrate:

**Lock and key model** — the active site is rigid and pre-formed to match the substrate exactly. Substrate slots in; reaction occurs; product leaves. Simple but doesn't account for the enzyme's flexibility.

**Induced fit model** (now accepted) — the active site is not perfectly rigid. When the substrate binds, the enzyme undergoes a conformational change, moulding itself more tightly around the substrate. This induced fit maximises the complementarity between enzyme and substrate, positioning reactive groups precisely for catalysis. The enzyme then springs back to its original shape when the product leaves.

The reaction occurs within the **enzyme-substrate (ES) complex**. The enzyme stabilises the transition state of the reaction, reducing the activation energy needed. Lower activation energy means a greater proportion of substrate molecules have sufficient energy to react at any given temperature.

## Factors Affecting the Rate of Enzyme-Controlled Reactions

### Temperature

Increasing temperature provides more kinetic energy to both enzyme and substrate molecules. This increases the **frequency of collisions** between enzyme and substrate, forming more ES complexes per unit time — rate increases.

However, above the **optimum temperature**, the increased thermal energy disrupts the hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic interactions maintaining the enzyme's tertiary structure. The active site changes shape — it is no longer complementary to the substrate. This is **denaturation**, and it is largely irreversible. Rate falls sharply. 

The optimum temperature for most human enzymes is approximately 37–40°C. For thermophilic bacteria (e.g. *Thermus aquaticus*, source of Taq polymerase in PCR), the optimum may be above 70°C.

### pH

Each enzyme has an **optimum pH** at which its active site has the correct shape and charge distribution. Moving away from the optimum alters the ionisation state of amino acid R groups — specifically the charged R groups (e.g. –NH₃⁺ and –COO⁻) involved in maintaining tertiary structure and forming the active site. This disrupts ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds, altering the shape of the active site. At extreme pH, denaturation occurs.

Example: pepsin (stomach protease) has an optimum pH of ~2; salivary amylase has an optimum of ~7.

### Substrate Concentration

With enzyme concentration fixed, increasing substrate concentration increases the rate of reaction because there are more substrate molecules available to collide with and bind to free active sites. More ES complexes form per unit time.

At low substrate concentration, many active sites are unoccupied — the enzyme is not working at full capacity. As [S] increases, active sites fill progressively. At very high substrate concentration, all active sites are occupied at any given moment — the enzyme is working at **maximum rate (V_max)**. Further increases in [S] produce no increase in rate because there are no free active sites. The enzyme is **saturated**.

### Enzyme Concentration

With substrate in excess, increasing enzyme concentration increases the rate because there are more active sites available. The rate is directly proportional to enzyme concentration until substrate becomes limiting.

## Enzyme Inhibitors

Inhibitors are molecules that reduce enzyme activity by interfering with the active site or the enzyme's overall shape.

### Competitive Inhibition

A **competitive inhibitor** has a shape similar enough to the substrate that it binds to the active site, occupying it and preventing the substrate from binding. It competes with the substrate for the same site.

- The inhibitor and substrate compete — if you increase substrate concentration enough, substrate can outcompete the inhibitor and occupy more active sites
- V_max is eventually **unchanged** if [S] is raised sufficiently
- The apparent K_m (substrate concentration needed for half V_max) is **increased** (more substrate needed to achieve the same rate)
- Example: malonate inhibits succinate dehydrogenase (competes with succinate in the Krebs cycle)

### Non-competitive Inhibition

A **non-competitive inhibitor** binds to a different site on the enzyme — the **allosteric site** — not the active site. This binding causes a conformational change in the enzyme's tertiary structure, which distorts the active site so it can no longer bind the substrate properly.

- Increasing substrate concentration does **not** overcome non-competitive inhibition — the substrate cannot displace the inhibitor from its separate site
- V_max is **reduced** (fewer active enzymes, even at saturating [S])
- K_m is unchanged (the affinity of functioning active sites for substrate is unaltered)
- Example: heavy metal ions (e.g. Pb²⁺, Hg²⁺) can act as non-competitive inhibitors by binding to –SH groups away from the active site

**End-product inhibition** (negative feedback) is a form of non-competitive inhibition used to control metabolic pathways: the final product of a pathway inhibits an earlier enzyme in that pathway, preventing overproduction of the product.

## Cofactors, Coenzymes and Prosthetic Groups

Many enzymes require additional non-protein molecules to function.

**Inorganic cofactors** — metal ions that bind to the enzyme (sometimes at or near the active site) and are essential for activity. Example: chloride ions (Cl⁻) activate amylase; zinc ions (Zn²⁺) are required by carbonic anhydrase.

**Coenzymes** — organic (non-protein) molecules that bind temporarily to the enzyme, often carrying chemical groups between enzymes in a pathway. They are not consumed — they are regenerated by other reactions. Examples:
- **NAD** and **FAD** (in respiration — carry hydrogen/electrons between reactions)
- **Coenzyme A** (carries acetyl groups in the link reaction and Krebs cycle)
- **NADP** (in photosynthesis — carries hydrogen/electrons for the Calvin cycle)

**Prosthetic groups** — non-protein components permanently (covalently) attached to the enzyme. Example: haem groups in catalase (the iron-containing centre that reacts with hydrogen peroxide).

Without their cofactor or coenzyme, enzymes are inactive (these inactive forms are called **apoenzymes**; the complete functional enzyme with its cofactor is a **holoenzyme**).

## Measuring Enzyme Activity

Enzyme activity can be measured by:
- Rate of product formation (e.g. O₂ production by catalase; glucose production from starch)
- Rate of substrate disappearance
- Colour change (e.g. iodine turning colourless as starch is broken down; DCPIP decolourisation)

Initial rate (rate at time zero) is used for comparisons because it avoids complications from product accumulation, substrate depletion, or inhibitor build-up.

## Summary

- **Enzymes** = globular protein catalysts; specific active site; lower activation energy; not consumed
- **Induced fit**: substrate binding causes conformational change in active site
- **Temperature**: ↑ temp → ↑ KE → ↑ collision frequency → ↑ rate; above optimum → denaturation → active site distorted → rate ↓
- **pH**: optimum pH for each enzyme; extremes denature by disrupting ionic bonds / H-bonds
- **[Substrate]**: ↑ [S] → more ES complexes → ↑ rate; at saturation (V_max) all active sites occupied — [S] has no further effect
- **[Enzyme]**: ↑ [E] → more active sites → ↑ rate (when [S] not limiting)
- **Competitive inhibitor**: binds active site; overcome by ↑ [S]; V_max unchanged; K_m ↑
- **Non-competitive inhibitor**: binds allosteric site; distorts active site; not overcome by ↑ [S]; V_max ↓
- **Cofactors/coenzymes**: inorganic ions or organic molecules required for enzyme activity (NAD, FAD, CoA are coenzymes)

## AQA Exam Tips

- **Denaturation language**: say the "active site changes shape / is no longer complementary to the substrate" — never just "broken down" or "destroyed." AQA rejects vague language.
- **Above optimum temperature**: AQA frequently distinguishes between "rate increases because more kinetic energy / more frequent collisions" (below optimum) and "rate decreases because denaturation / active site changes shape" (above optimum). Both parts need molecular detail.
- **Competitive vs non-competitive**: know the effect on V_max and K_m, and be able to interpret graphs. Key test: if adding more substrate restores rate → competitive. If not → non-competitive.
- **Do not say enzymes add heat or provide energy**: enzymes lower activation energy by providing an alternative reaction pathway via the ES complex — not by adding energy.
- **Saturation**: when [S] is very high and rate plateaus, the explanation is always "all active sites are occupied / enzyme is saturated — no free active sites available."
- **Cofactors in exam context**: AQA often asks why an enzyme is inactive in the absence of a cofactor. Answer: the cofactor is needed to maintain the correct shape of the active site (or to carry chemical groups in the reaction).
